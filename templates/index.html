Purpose and Mission:

As medical professionals, prescribers are constantly making cost-benefit analysis regarding the potential benefits and downsides of a certain medication for patients. However, treatment is never a guarantee for a cure. Age, gender, genetics, and many other aspects of life are uncontrollable. It is at the discretion of the physician to provide care to the best of their ability, but human error and pharmaceutical marketing has caused a severe clouding of this judgement.

Overprescribing, in general health care and specific cases, is an issue that adversely affects the quality of life and health of millions of Americans. This issue is often overlooked as the rapid provision of medication for symptom alleviation is often prioritized over adverse effects and long term health implications.

In 2018, 46% of a half a million of outpatient antibiotic prescriptions were written without any diagnosis of infection. In 2019, among the top 10 most frequently prescribed medication classes for US adults, four were found for mass overprescribing. These included medications such as opioids for chronic pain, proton pump inhibitors for indigestion, levothyroxine for subclinical hypothyroidism, and antidepressants for subsyndromal levels of depression, all of which have severe adverse reactions that can be life debilitating or ending if taking improperly or by the improper patient.


These problems also adversely affect certain groups, where older adults over age 65 take five or more medications, often resulting in adverse drug effects that compounding to the patient suffering already caused by the original disease being treated.

However, due to lax prescribing guidelines, lack of longitudinal studies on adverse effects, and aggressive marketing tactics by pharmaceutical companies, the medical system has widely neglected due diligence in dispensing prescriptions. These three issues are at the core of this problem.

In combination with these compounding influences, patients often do not perform their own research about medications they take. These chemicals and substances have profound and significant effects on the systems vital for human functioning everyday. It is imparative that patients are fully educated to make proper decisions about their treatment plan. The reasoning is twofold: First, only the patient truly understands their ailments and health as they are the only individual able to experience their sickness. If they are not knowledgeable about the substances used for treatment, it may be impossible to establish relationships between the administration of the medication and changes in symptomology. Second, though health care professionals, especially doctors and certified physicians, are among the most trusted and intelligent members of the community, they are all still human. This is not to say that the medical community is untrustworthy by any means, but rather that mistakes unfortunately do occur. Therefore information provided by a physician should be taken as medical advice and not instruction. When making decisions that have major effects on your health, especially when condsidering medications that may alter how the brain works, it is always necessary to perform your own research and seek opinions from multiple medical professionals, preferrably providers that specialize in the ailments at hand.

A written prescription is not an obligation.

So take a second to think, before you take one.
